Grammar and usage

It pays to be vigilant to the most common (and unpardonable) grammar pitfalls in your sentence constructions.

That vs which

  • Always use which to introduce (or define) a non-restrictive clause.

  • Use that for definitions or restrictive clauses.

Subject-verb agreement

When the subject of a sentence is composed of two or more nouns or pronouns separated by and, use a plural verb The gear and sprocket are part of the same assembly.
When the subject of a sentence is composed of two or more nouns or pronouns separated by oror nor, use a singular verb Neither the pen nor the sword is mightier than the word.
When a compound subject is formed by both a singular and a plural noun or pronoun, the verb should agree with the part of the subject that is closer to the verb

Dangling participles and misplaced modifiers

A word that ends in -ing often introduces a dangling modifier. When a phrase or word has no object that it can logically modify, it is called a dangling modifier.
